What is a Common Stock?



In simple terms, a common stock is a share of ownership in a public company.

When you buy a stock, you're buying a small piece of that company, giving you a claim on its assets and a share of its profits.

It's the most basic way for companies to raise money and for people to invest in their growth.

Common vs. Preferred Stock

Companies issue two main types of stock. While both represent ownership, they offer different rights and rewards. Here's a look at the key differences.

Common Stock

  • ✔
    Voting Rights: Usually have the power to vote on company decisions, like electing the board of directors.
  • ~
    Dividends: Not guaranteed. Paid out only if the company decides to, and the amount can change.
  • ✔
    Growth Potential: Unlimited. The stock price can rise significantly, leading to high returns.
  • ✖
    Liquidation Priority: Last in line. In case of bankruptcy, they get paid only after everyone else (creditors, preferred stockholders).

Preferred Stock

  • ✖
    Voting Rights: Typically have no voting power in the company.
  • ✔
    Dividends: Priority payments. Usually receive fixed, regular dividend payments before common stockholders.
  • ~
    Growth Potential: Limited. Price is more stable, acting more like a bond, with lower potential for high growth.
  • ✔
    Liquidation Priority: Paid before common stockholders, but still after debt holders.

The Owner's Toolkit: Rights & Rewards

Owning a stock is more than just a number on a screen. It comes with a set of rights that give you influence and ways to earn a return on your investment. Explore the core concepts below.

Voting Power

Influence company direction by voting on major issues like electing board members and approving mergers. It's your say in how the company is run.

Claim to Dividends

Receive a portion of the company's profits, if the board decides to distribute them. This provides a potential income stream from your investment.

Transfer Ownership

Freely buy and sell your shares on stock exchanges like the NYSE or NASDAQ. This liquidity allows you to convert your investment to cash.

Inspect Documents

Right to review key corporate documents, including financial reports (10-K, 10-Q), to stay informed about the company's health and performance.

Sue for Wrongful Acts

A legal right to sue the company for misconduct, such as fraud or misleading investors. This serves as a crucial form of investor protection.

Pre-emptive Rights

The opportunity to buy new shares before they are offered to the public, allowing you to maintain your percentage of ownership in the company.

Investors make money in two main ways: the stock's price going up (capital appreciation) or the company paying out profits (dividends). The balance depends on the type of company. Toggle between a "Growth" and "Income" stock to see the difference.

Understanding the Risks

High potential rewards come with high potential risks. The most critical risk is your place in the pecking order if a company fails. This is called liquidation priority.

1. Secured Creditors

First in line. These are lenders with claims backed by specific assets (e.g., banks with mortgages). They get paid first from the sale of those assets.

2. Unsecured Creditors

Paid after secured creditors. This includes suppliers, bondholders, and employees owed wages. Their claims are not backed by specific assets.

3. Preferred Stockholders

Paid after all creditors. They have priority over common stockholders and are often guaranteed a specific payout amount.

4. Common Stockholders

Last in line. They receive whatever is left over, which is often nothing. This "residual claim" is the source of their high risk.

This hierarchy shows why common stock is the riskiest investment in a company's capital structure. You share in the greatest upside, but also face the greatest potential loss.
